Behavioral failure · Mar–Apr 2026 · stop-only-on-real-risk rule origin

Selling the winner, then buying it back higher

Micron (MU) was the book's highest-conviction position — a structural memory-cycle thesis that kept confirming. On Mar 12 2026, with the position up strongly, the system trimmed 5 shares at $420 with the classic justification: "lock profits, raise cash, manage risk."

What happened next

DateEventPrice
2026-03-12TRIM 5 shares — profit-lock reflex$420
2026-04-14ADD back +5 — thesis intact all along$435
2026-04-16ADD +3$452
2026-04-23ADD +5$480
2026-04-29Position up >100% on cost$521

Net effect: paid commissions to sell at $420 and re-buy the same exposure at $435–$480. No dated bearish signal existed on Mar 12 — no earnings risk, no sector break, no thesis change. The trim was a feeling, not a signal.

The three-question gate that came out of it

  1. Is the position a healthy winner? If yes, default is no stop and no trim — the position is doing what it was bought to do.
  2. Is there a dated, named bearish signal within 30 days? "Feels overbought" does not qualify; "customer capex print Thursday, miss gaps this 10–15%" does.
  3. Would the signal's expected move actually reach the proposed stop? A stop 27% below price does not protect against a 12% event move — it is a thesis exit mislabeled as a hedge.
Governance fix (stop-only-on-real-risk rule): profit-locking stops and trims on healthy winners are mechanically flagged. Applied retroactively to the prior ten positions, the gate blocked all four historical churn decisions and none of the winning ones.

The receipts make the cost auditable: the TRIM row, the three ADD rows above the trim price, and the rule's arrival date are all in the chain. Compounding is the product; churn is the tax the ledger now surfaces.
