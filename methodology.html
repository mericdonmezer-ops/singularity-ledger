Methodology

The smallest useful loop: decision → receipt → outcome → grade → policy change.

Two tiers

  • LIVE — first published while the outcome was still unknown. This is the verifiable tier.
  • ARCHIVE — backfilled from internal logs after outcomes were knowable. Real history, not pre-registration.

How a customer would use it

  1. Your agent emits a structured receipt at the moment it makes a consequential decision.
  2. The receipt captures the claim, authority tier, evidence reference, and resolution window — not a vague chat transcript.
  3. The ledger seals the record with a hash chain. Published claims cannot be silently rewritten.
  4. When the window closes, a resolver grades the outcome and exposes calibration, misses, and policy regressions.

Scoring

  • Forecasts — Brier score on resolved probability calls. Below 0.18 = good; above 0.25 = not earned yet.
  • Rejections — forward return on names we said no to (20d window).
  • Exits — post-sell drift vs thesis (good exit vs churn).

Why this is useful

Agent teams already have traces, evals, and observability. Decision Ledger covers the gap after an agent says “do this”: was it allowed to decide, was the claim registered before the result, did it resolve, and did the team change policy when it failed?

Publish QC

Build fails on empty claims, placeholders, encoding errors, stale source rows, and integrity mismatches. The public reference instance also scrubs private account details before publication.

Integrity

Claim hash covers immutable fields (id, type, date, ticker, statement, probability, resolve-by). Resolution fields may update; claims may not. Git history of the public repo is a second tamper-evidence layer.
